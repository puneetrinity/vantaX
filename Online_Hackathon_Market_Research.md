## 1. The Online Hackathon Landscape

Online hackathons have evolved from weekend coding sprints into structured talent discovery, brand-building, and innovation engines. In 2025–2026, they're used by corporations, startups, academic institutions, and government bodies to crowdsource ideas, test prototypes, and build talent pipelines.

The Indian ecosystem is particularly active. Platforms like Unstop alone list 2,500+ hackathons, and government initiatives like Smart India Hackathon (SIH) run at massive national scale. The market is crowded — but the opportunity is real if you differentiate on execution and post-event value.

**Key market trends:**
- AI/ML-themed hackathons are the fastest-growing category
- Individual participation formats are rising alongside traditional team models
- Post-event talent pipeline is becoming more valuable than the event itself
- Paid entry hackathons exist but are the minority; most rely on sponsors
- 7-day build windows are replacing 24–48 hour sprint formats for online events

- Enterprise-focused hackathon platform with rich developer community

## 3. How Online Hackathons Are Structured

### Typical Flow (What the Market Does)

```
Registration → Pre-Event Warmup → Opening Ceremony → Build Period → Submission → Judging → Results
```

**Phase 1: Registration (4–6 weeks before)**
- Most use Devfolio or Unstop for registration + payment
- Fields typically collected: Name, Email, College, Year, Skills, GitHub, LinkedIn
- Team formation happens on-platform or via Discord
- Many offer early bird pricing or free registration with sponsor backing

**Phase 2: Pre-Event Engagement (1–2 weeks before)**
- Welcome email with important details sent immediately after registration
- Pre-event workshops, webinars, or ideation sessions
- Discord/WhatsApp community setup for networking
- Mentor matching or office hours announced
- Template sharing, past examples, and challenge briefs released early

**Phase 3: Opening Ceremony (Day 1)**
- Typically via Zoom or YouTube Live
- Problem statements/challenges released
- Rules, judging criteria, and timeline explained
- Judges and mentors introduced
- Q&A session

**Phase 4: Build Period**
- Duration varies widely:
  - Sprint format: 24–48 hours (traditional, higher energy, higher drop-off)
  - Extended format: 5–7 days (better for online, allows deeper work)
  - Multi-phase: Idea submission → shortlist → build → demo (used by SIH)
- Support provided via Discord channels, mentor office hours, FAQ documents
- Some platforms offer mid-event check-ins or mini-contests to maintain engagement

**Phase 5: Submission**
- Project submitted on Devfolio/Unstop/Devpost with:
  - Project description and problem statement
  - Tech stack used
  - Demo video (typically 2–3 minutes)
  - GitHub repository link
  - Live demo URL (if applicable)
  - Presentation/slides
- Backup submission via Google Drive link is common for smaller hackathons

**Phase 6: Judging (3–7 days)**
- Round 1: Technical screening to verify eligibility and basic quality
- Round 2: Detailed review by domain experts using scoring rubrics
- Round 3 (optional): Live demo presentations for top 10–15 shortlisted projects
- Common scoring: 1–5 stars per criterion on platforms like Devpost

**Phase 7: Results & Post-Event**
- Live winner announcement via stream
- Certificates distributed within 48 hours
- Post-event engagement: community groups, internship pipelines, upsells

## 4. Format Comparison: What Works for Online

| Format | Duration | Engagement | Drop-off Risk | Best For |
|--------|----------|------------|---------------|----------|
| 24-hour Sprint | 1 day | Very high | Medium | Experienced hackers, hype events |
| 48-hour Sprint | 2 days | High | Medium-High | Traditional hackathon feel |
| 5–7 Day Build | 1 week | Moderate | High (needs active management) | Students, working professionals |
| Multi-Phase | 2–4 weeks | Variable | Medium | Large-scale, government hackathons |

**Your blueprint uses the 7-day format** — this is good for fresh grads who have classes/jobs, but you MUST actively manage engagement throughout the week. The biggest risk with extended online formats is registration-to-submission drop-off, which can be as high as 60–70% without active engagement.

## 6. Judging Frameworks Used in the Market

### Common Criteria (Industry Standard)

| Criterion | Typical Weight | What Judges Look For |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| Innovation/Creativity | 20–30% | Novel approach, unique solution, fresh thinking |
| Technical Execution | 20–30% | Code quality, architecture, use of tools/APIs |
| Practical Usability | 15–25% | Does it solve a real problem? Is it usable? |
| Business Viability | 10–20% | Market potential, scalability, feasibility |
| Presentation/Demo | 10–20% | Clear communication, compelling demo video |

**Your blueprint uses 25% equal weighting across 4 criteria** (Innovation, Usability, Execution, Presentation). This is clean and transparent, which is good. Most platforms use a similar balanced approach.

### Judge Selection Best Practices
- Diverse panel: technology + business + design backgrounds
- 5–7 judges is the sweet spot (your blueprint targets minimum 5)
- Brief judges beforehand with a scoring rubric and calibration session
- Use structured scoring sheets (1–5 or 1–10 scale per criterion)
- Have backup judges ready (your risk matrix already covers this)

### Submission Requirements (Market Standard)
- 2–3 minute demo video (not a presentation — a working demo)
- GitHub repository link
- Project writeup (problem, solution, tech stack, challenges)
- Live demo URL when possible
- Some hackathons also require a pitch deck (3–5 slides)

## 7. Engagement & Drop-off: The Critical Challenge

### The Drop-off Problem
Online hackathons face a fundamental engagement challenge. Industry data suggests:
- **Registration to attendance:** 50–70% show up for opening ceremony
- **Attendance to submission:** 30–50% actually submit a project
- **Net registration-to-submission rate:** Often as low as 20–35%

For a 7-day online event targeting 800+ registrations, you might see only 200–350 actual submissions without active intervention.

### What Top Hackathons Do to Reduce Drop-off

**Pre-event (2 weeks before):**
- Immediate welcome email after registration with timeline, Discord invite, and prep resources
- Weekly reminder emails with countdown and tips
- Pre-event ideation workshop or webinar to build commitment
- Team formation support (even for individual events, community building matters)

**During event:**
- Daily check-in prompts via WhatsApp/Discord
- Mini-contests or milestone challenges (e.g., "submit your idea sketch by Day 2")
- Live Q&A sessions with mentors or judges
- Leaderboard or progress tracker (if platform supports it)
- Peer showcase — let participants share progress with each other

**Submission deadline:**
- 48-hour and 24-hour deadline reminders
- "Incomplete submission" prompts for registered but inactive participants
- Extended grace period for technical issues (backup Google Drive link)

### Your Blueprint Gap
Your current plan has the 7-day window but doesn't detail daily engagement touchpoints. This is the single biggest risk area. Consider adding:
- A daily challenge or micro-task structure (not just 3 challenges across the week)
- WhatsApp broadcast engagement at fixed times
- A "Day 3 checkpoint" where participants share work-in-progress

### Key Observation
Most established hackathons in India use **Devfolio or Unstop as the primary platform** — this handles registration, submission, and judging in one place. Your blueprint uses a custom stack (separate landing page + payment gateway + submission portal). This gives you more control and data ownership, but also means more integration work and a higher platform crash risk.

**Consideration:** Even if you run the event on your own infrastructure, listing it on Unstop or Devfolio for discovery/marketing is a common strategy. Many organizers use these platforms purely for visibility while hosting submissions elsewhere.

### The Discord vs WhatsApp Decision
- **Discord** is the global standard for hackathon communities (channels, threads, voice rooms, bot integrations)
- **WhatsApp** is where Indian college students actually live — higher open rates, lower friction
- Your choice of WhatsApp (AiSensy) is smart for this audience, but consider a Discord server as a secondary channel for those who prefer it

2. ₹199 pricing — accessible, filters out non-serious participants
3. 3-track challenge structure — gives participants choice without overwhelming them

5. Risk management matrix with fallback plans

### What to Add or Improve

7. **Consider a Discord server alongside WhatsApp** — WhatsApp for announcements, Discord for community and technical support
